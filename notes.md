# Revision notes: reviewer comments paired with our answers

Working notes for the major revision of "A Generic Residue-Numbering System for Microbial Rhodopsins" (Hidber et al., npj Structural Biology, submission 6d03d7a6-f89e-4a33-8c5e-393c7acf9fba, deadline 29 Apr 2026). Each reviewer point is reproduced verbatim from `reviewer_notes.md`, followed by the internal response recorded in that file and then a notes block that states what can be substantiated directly from code or from the reference table in this repository, what cannot, and what the concrete answer to the reviewer should be. The document is organised so that any quoted phrase from the reviewer letter can be found by text search.

## Editor letter

The journal classified the manuscript as major revision on the basis of two reviewer reports that both acknowledge scientific merit but raise significant concerns. The editor explicitly flagged one question as important: "how will such a system be implemented." That question is answered by the webservice we are now building and by the companion supplementary section in `supplementary_grn_annotation.md`; everything else in this document is the point-by-point material that must accompany the webservice in the rebuttal package.

## Reviewer 1

Their report is largely constructive and their main ask is a more extensive analysis of the newly identified, previously uncharacterised clusters. They also ask for softer wording on RMSD values, a self-contained explanation of the MO numbering system, and three very small corrections in abstract and introduction.

### R1 major 1 — characterisation of uncharacterised clusters

Reviewer text: "The manuscript would benefit from more extended preliminary analysis of the newly identified, previously uncharacterized clusters. Specifically, the authors should more discuss: Conserved amino acid residues characteristic of each new cluster. Whether these residues are unique to the respective cluster. Their positions within the predicted structural models. Possible functional implications based on their structural context. Such analysis would significantly strengthen the biological relevance of the study."

Team response in `reviewer_notes.md`: Xavi considers this easy to address and proposes adding the analysis at the end of the "Application of the MO numbering system to a large dataset of microbial rhodopsins" section. Hideaki committed Seiya to generating additional figures that highlight conserved amino acid residues, map them onto predicted structural models, and offer cautious functional interpretation (pump, channel, regulator, or possibly novel class).

Our notes: substantiable from data. The conserved-residue analysis is a column-wise computation over rows of `protos/src/protos/reference_data/grn/reference/type_I.csv` restricted to each cluster. The canonical GRN columns are already aligned across all 129 entries, so the analysis reduces to per-cluster amino-acid frequency and entropy at each column. The "uniqueness" check is the same computation against the complementary set of clusters. Mapping onto predicted models uses the AlphaFold outputs already curated for the reference table. The only thing this requires that the repository does not yet contain explicitly is the cluster-assignment vector itself — whatever labelling produced the "uncharacterised" clusters in the current draft needs to be surfaced so the analysis can be rerun. Action: ask Seiya for the cluster labels, then generate the conserved-residue figure per cluster.

### R1 major 2 — RMSD wording

Reviewer text: '"The predicted models have high accuracy. Models in benchmark set A had a mean backbone Cα RMSD of 0.61 ± 0.23 Å… Models in blind set B had a mean backbone Cα RMSD of 0.80 ± 0.24 Å… The mean differences between the predicted models and the reference structures remain below 1 Å." In general, RMSD values in the range of 0.5–0.8 Å (±0.3 Å) should not automatically be described as "high accuracy." There are two reasons for this: even sub-angstrom differences may affect the presence or absence of specific hydrogen bonds, which are often critical for rhodopsin function. The reported RMSD values are averages over entire structures. Functionally important regions may exhibit significantly larger deviations despite modest global RMSD values. Fortunately, the RMSD values do not appear essential for the main conclusions of the manuscript and do not compromise the overall analysis. Nevertheless, I recommend replacing the term "high" with a more precise descriptor (e.g., "good," "reasonable," or "sub-angstrom agreement")."'

Team response: Hideaki agreed it is a wording change.

Our notes: not substantiable from code — this is a pure wording fix. Replace "high accuracy" with "sub-angstrom agreement" throughout. If we wanted to address R1's subsidiary concern that functional regions might deviate more than the global mean, we would have to recompute per-region RMSDs from the benchmark outputs; this is optional given that R1 already accepts that RMSDs are not central to the conclusions. The minimum-effort answer is the wording change, and it is what R1 asked for.

### R1 major 3 — explanation of the generic residue-numbering system

A concise description similar to that provided in the original work by Ballesteros JA and Weinstein H (Methods in Neurosciences, 1995) would be helpful. Team response: Xavi argued that the existing text — "MO numbering codes consist of two numbers: the first denotes the helix (1-7) and the second the position within the helix relative to the residue closest to the retinal, defined as position 50. Our notes: substantiable from data for the worked example that accompanies the figure. HsBR `1c3w` gives 3.50=T90, 4.50=M118, 5.50=S141, 6.50=W182, 7.50=K216 when its row is read from `type_I.csv`. The textual explanation can be expanded to state explicitly (a) that 7.50 is by construction the Schiff-base lysine, (b) that the other x.50 positions were placed at conserved, functionally central residues near the retinal, and (c) that positions increase toward the C-terminus and decrease toward the N-terminus within each helix. This directly addresses reviewer 2's separate complaint about anchor ambiguity (see R2 major 1 below) using the same figure.

## Reviewer 2

Reviewer 2's report is the harder of the two. Xavi flagged in `reviewer_notes.md` that the reviewer is probably Peter Hegemann and that despite the severity of the critique the reviewer explicitly writes that the work "will lead to the complete switch of how we see the rhodopsin structure and how we do analysis and write papers", which is a strong compliment and a signal that the reviewer is taking the review seriously rather than dismissing the work. ### R2 summary — narrow to channelrhodopsins

Reviewer text: 'In this paragraph there is a summary of my Review, and below are the detailed critics. In brief, while I fully agree that comparison of different subfamilies or clades within the microbial rhodopsin family is difficult and requires a lot of knowledge, I''m not convinced that such comparison should be done in the first place. To give an example, there is no relevance of comparing HsBR to Tara-RRB, for instance, as they are the proteins from different worlds. The unification of the numbering of their residues is simply not needed. Therefore, I couldn''t see the significance of the existing problem, which the authors tried to resolve. Yet, from the text and my expertise I could identify a certain field where the problem indeed exists - channelrhodopsins. I think narrowing the proposed scheme to channelrhodopsins will address the main issues and also facilitate the methodology.'

Team response: Xavi disagreed and cited the original Tara-RRB paper (Rozenberg et al., Nat. Struct. Mol. Biol. 2022) in which Tara-RRB is explicitly compared to HsBR (Figure 4b of that paper). Hideaki pointed out that reviewer 1 asks for the opposite — broader application to uncharacterised clusters — and proposed asking the editor for guidance before committing to a revision direction.

Our notes: not substantiable from code directly, but substantiable by literature. The Rozenberg 2022 citation is the strongest direct counter-example. A second class of counter-examples is the fluid pump/channel boundary: point mutations that convert pumps into channels or vice versa (this is standard rhodopsin literature and can be cited). The rebuttal should concede that narrow within-clade numberings are valuable and co-exist naturally with our scheme (the E1-E5 system for ChRs is a good example), but maintain that a unified scheme is necessary for exactly the kind of large-scale dataset comparison the manuscript demonstrates. The strategic recommendation — asking the editor before finalising direction — remains the correct move given that R1 and R2 pull in opposite directions.

### R2 major 1 — anchor identification

Reviewer text: "The major point is in how the anchors are identified. From Fig. 4b (it misses the label of Y axis), I can see that not a single, but several residues are equally distant from the retinal in helices TM3, TM5, TM6. How exactly is the X.50 residue then chosen? And what if the microbial rhodopsin lacks retinal, yet maintains the fold? We know examples of non-K, or flotillin-associated rhodopsins. Team response: Xavi acknowledged the ambiguity and compared it to the Ballesteros-Weinstein choice of Pro over Tyr in TM5 — that is, when several candidates are near-equidistant, the authors make a consistent choice. He argued that this needs better explanation in the text, and that users do not need to model the opsin or measure distances themselves because annotation can be done by sequence alignment to an already-annotated reference. Hideaki said the reviewer misunderstood and that the methodology actually integrates both retinal proximity and sequence conservation, and that the workflow figure is essential to clarify this.

Our notes: partially substantiable from code, with one real gap. First, the workflow figure is substantiable via the HsBR values already extracted — 3.50=T90, 4.50=M118, 5.50=S141, 6.50=W182, 7.50=K216 — because K216 and W182 are textbook-level conserved, functionally central residues. The figure can use HsBR as the worked example and show side-by-side that (a) the residues are close to retinal and (b) they are sequence-conserved, which is exactly the dual criterion the rebuttal claims. Second, the rhodopsins-without-retinal question is substantiable because the webservice annotates any query by sequence alignment to the nearest reference (see `supplementary_grn_annotation.md`); this is a demonstration that retinal is not required at runtime.

The real gap, and the single most important hole to close before resubmission: the runtime code in `protos/src/protos/processing/grn/` does NOT re-derive anchors. It propagates them from the reference table. The original anchor-selection procedure — the one-off curation that placed the x.50 positions on the 129 reference rhodopsins, using retinal proximity and sequence conservation — does not exist as a script in the repository. It lives in notes, in earlier drafts, and in expert memory. For the rebuttal to be credible, the procedure must be written up as a Methods subsection (or a supplementary note), ideally with the HsBR worked example carried through. Without it, a careful reviewer reading the code will not find the dual-criterion claim implemented and the rebuttal risks collapsing.

### R2 — color-tuning residues not being anchors

Reviewer text: 'This becomes even more misleading, and, in my opinion, self-contradictory, in this sentence of the "Application of the generic residue-numbering system" section: "Residues at positions 3.53, 4.51, 6.54, and 7.49—known as the L/Q, N/LI, G/P, and A/TS switches, respectively—contribute to spectral tuning in microbial rhodopsins30, 31, 32, 33.". One would expect that the closest residue to the retinal (anchor) is the most responsible for color tuning. Yet, in the referenced literature, none of the listed residues are anchors in MO numbering, which questions the selection of anchors itself.'

Team response: Xavi argued that anchors provide spatial reference, not functional reference, and that it is normal that functional residues are not necessarily at x.50. He noted that 3.53, 4.51, 6.54 and 7.49 are also near the retinal and could have been chosen as anchors, and asked whether there was a reason they were not. Hideaki argued that the reviewer misunderstood the anchor-selection logic and that color-tuning residues and anchors play different roles — 1.50 and 2.50 are not color-tuning residues because TM1 and TM2 are far from retinal, while 3.50, 4.50, 5.50 are critical for color tuning at least in channelrhodopsins; 6.50 is so structurally essential that mutation compromises protein integrity and therefore published color-tuning data at 6.50 are sparse for experimental reasons, not because the position is uninvolved; and 3.53 (the L/Q switch) tunes the spectrum despite not directly contacting retinal.

Our notes: substantiable from data, both structurally and in the reference table. The positions 3.53, 4.51, 6.54 and 7.49 that R2 cites are all canonical columns in `type_I.csv` (verified by inspection of the column header), so the scheme already accommodates the residues R2 highlights — they are just not anchors. The distance-to-retinal question can be answered quantitatively by measuring these positions versus the x.50 positions in the PDB files shipped in `protos/` (1u19, 1c3w, 2z73, 3pqr, 1s81). The rebuttal paragraph can be structured as: (i) anchors provide a coordinate system and are placed at the position in each helix most useful as a reference point — this happens to combine proximity to retinal with sequence conservation and in several cases with direct functional centrality (7.50 is the Schiff-base lysine); (ii) color-tuning residues are not all anchors for the same reason that not every functionally important residue in a GPCR is a Ballesteros-Weinstein x.50; (iii) concretely, 3.53, 4.51, 6.54 and 7.49 are canonical GRN columns in our scheme and are freely citeable; they just are not the x.50 of their helices. This answer neutralises the "self-contradictory" framing by distinguishing coordinate reference from functional annotation.

### R2 — TM and boundary determination, E194 example

Reviewer text: 'Next, the question is how the TM itself is determined. While this does not influence much the residues of the rental binding pocket, the ion-selectivity filters are often located close to the tips of the helices, and determination of loop regions and helix-to-loop transition regions is a question. It relies on structural data quality (both experimental and predictions), but also can depend on conditions (pH, salt, technique - crystal contacts or special detergent interactions in cryoEM). This will result in different numbering. A great example is given by authors, in the following sentence: "Upon photoactivation, the proton is released from the Schiff base to the extracellular bulk solvent via T3.49, D3.45, and the proton release group composed of EECL3 and E7.38, accompanied by a conformational change of the key arginine residue R3.42. " Here, the residue EECL3, which is a part of proton release group of HsBR, E194, is placed in the ECL3 loop, while, arguably, it belongs to the distorted helical part of TM6. Also, what if the ECL3 contains several glutamates? How is the numbering performed in this case? I don''t see benefits of adding more labels or numbers in Figures and text. Absolute numbering system (such as E194) does not leave place for errors, why should it be supplemented / replaced by something that ambiguous?'

Team response in `reviewer_notes.md`: Xavi noted that loop annotation is a general problem for all GRN schemes (including Ballesteros-Weinstein), and that "absolute residue numbering" is fine for within-family work but fails for family-wide comparisons, which is the problem the paper addresses. Flurin committed to rewriting the description of loops and end-of-helix regions. Hideaki suggested the web server could include a function to generate sequence alignments with secondary structure annotations, similar to GPCRdb.

Our notes: substantiable from code and data. The TM boundaries used by the runtime propagation are encoded in `protos/src/protos/reference_data/grn/configs/` (config.json, binding_domain2.json) and are therefore explicit, fixed, and inspectable. The specific E194 example in HsBR can be read off directly from the `1c3w` row of `type_I.csv` — whatever label the reference table assigns to E194 is what the manuscript is using, and stating it explicitly ("our reference table assigns E194 to position X") makes the answer auditable. For the rebuttal: concede that boundary definition at the TM/loop transition is a modelling choice and not a unique answer, explain that in this manuscript the boundaries are fixed once, deterministically, for all 129 reference structures based on secondary-structure assignment (DSSP/STRIDE), and that downstream annotation is fully reproducible from that choice. Flurin's rewrite should state (a) which secondary-structure tool was used, (b) at which confidence threshold, and (c) how the transition residue was assigned when tool and visual inspection disagreed.

### R2 — gaps and insertions

Reviewer text: '"In this case, the ''missing'' position (6.48) is simply skipped.". This would mean that in the proteins with indeed distinct structural features, the numbering system will fail in the region of difference - then what is the point of introducing this system to facilitate the comparison of distant clades? The solution of skipping the numbers in case of gaps or insertions is in no way easier than manual analysis of the structural alignment and has to be reviewed in each particular case; therefore, I don''t see how it is useful.'

Team response: Xavi cited the Isberg 2015 paper (Trends in Pharmacological Sciences) as the precedent for the GPCR rule set and said we need to explain better how it is done and show more examples. Hideaki argued that skipping a number or adding a digit is not a failure but a feature: a skipped position signals a 3_10-like compression relative to the canonical fold, and an inserted digit (e.g. 3.521) signals a π-helix-like extension.

Our notes: substantiable from code. The rules are implemented deterministically in `protos/src/protos/processing/grn/grn_assignment.py` (`_annotate_missing_rns`, lines 297-364): insertions within a helix receive a three-decimal suffix on the nearest flanking GRN; inter-helical loops receive a two-digit region label plus distance-to-anchor suffix; missing canonical positions are left unassigned and manifest as a skipped number. The webservice supplementary (`supplementary_grn_annotation.md`) already describes these rules in publishable form. Concrete examples of both cases can be harvested from `type_I.csv` and included in the main-text figure or supplement. The rebuttal should make Hideaki's point explicitly: far from being an ambiguity, the skipping and inserting behaviour is the information channel that makes structural deviations visible in the numbering itself, and it is applied deterministically by our software rather than by case-by-case manual judgement.

### R2 — three-letter motifs, E1-E5, narrowing to ChRs again

Reviewer text: 'As stated by the Authors in Discussion: "The three-residue TM3 motif introduced in 2013-2014 provides a useful signature for classifying ion-pump functions24, 25; however, in channelrhodopsins, functional properties are determined primarily by other residues, including five glutamates in TM2, rather than by the TM3 motif." The existing system of 3-letter or 6-letter motifs works well for ion-pumping rhodopsins. Yet, it fails for ChRs - which makes sense and I agree with authors that ChRs should be structured differently. Therefore, I see the usefulness of the special numbering system for ChRs - but I doubt that MO numbering is the best solution, as it is designed for a broader variety of microbial rhodopsins. For instance, when considering the sentence: "Instead, ion selectivity and kinetics are largely determined by five glutamates (E1–E5, corresponding to E2.46, E2.47, E2.54, E2.61, and E2.65) aligned along TM249, 50, 51, 52", it appears to me that there is already a special system for ChRs where glutamates are assigned as E1 to E5. Looking in Fig. 5d it becomes hard to read the labels as they contain 3 different numberings.'

Team response: Xavi explained that each researcher can pick which of the three numberings to display (e.g. E1212.46 vs E2.46 vs E2.46) and that the authors deliberately include all three in Fig. 5d so readers know exactly which residue is meant regardless of their preferred convention. Hideaki noted that this is essentially the same narrow-to-ChRs suggestion as R2's summary, and added a Hegemann-identity caution: if the reviewer is Peter Hegemann (who proposed the E1-E5 system), we must not call that system "imperfect" in the response, but we can legitimately note that it does not generalise to anion-conducting or trimeric channelrhodopsins.

Our notes: substantiable from data, and this is arguably our strongest concrete rebuttal. The reference table `type_I.csv` contains canonical columns for all five of the E1-E5 positions (2.46, 2.47, 2.54, 2.61, 2.65). Reading the cation-conducting ChR entries off the table: C1C2 (`3ug9`) carries E121, E122, E129, E136, E140 at those positions; 6eid carries E82, E83, E90, E97, E101; CnChR2 (`cnchr2_j230_refine9`) carries E121, E122, E129, E136, E140. In cation-conducting ChRs the five positions are canonical columns 2.46, 2.47, 2.54, 2.61 and 2.65 and are consistently occupied by glutamates, as shown directly in our reference table [examples]." The cluttered-label critique of Fig. 5d can be addressed either by accepting Xavi's explanation in the caption or by dropping the redundant absolute labels in the figure and moving them to the caption instead. On the narrowing question itself, the rebuttal should note that E1-E5 works cleanly for dimeric cation-conducting ChRs, does not apply to anion ChRs or trimeric ChRs, and therefore is not a universal solution for the diversity the paper covers. Hegemann-identity caution stands: do not use the word "imperfect" for E1-E5 in the response.

## Cross-cutting items

### Scope conflict between R1 and R2

R1 asks for broader application (more uncharacterised clusters, more analysis). R2 asks to narrow to channelrhodopsins. ### Anchor-selection methodology — the gap to close

This is the single most important item to close before resubmission. The runtime code in `protos/src/protos/processing/grn/` does not re-derive anchors; it propagates them from the curated reference table. The reference table was built once, manually, using retinal proximity and sequence conservation. That procedure does not exist as a script in this repository. For the rebuttal to be credible, the procedure must be written up as a Methods subsection or a supplementary note, with (a) the criteria used, (b) how ties were broken when several residues were near-equidistant, (c) the HsBR worked example, and (d) any deliberate deviations made for specific helices. Without this, reviewer 2's anchor-ambiguity critique cannot be fully answered and the dual-criterion claim made in our response will not match what a code-reading reviewer would find. Seiya's workflow figure should be paired with this Methods subsection, and the subsection should precede the figure in the manuscript.
